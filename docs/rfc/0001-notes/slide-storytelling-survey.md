# Slide Storytelling & Executive Presentation: Survey

> Topic: slide-storytelling
> Mode: standard
> Date: 2026-07-09

---

## Scope

This survey covers the major practitioner frameworks and available research on storytelling in slide presentations, with particular attention to executive and C-suite communication. Five parallel retrieval threads covered: Minto/SCQA, McKinsey deck conventions, Nancy Duarte (Resonate/Sparkline), Garr Reynolds (Presentation Zen), and executive communication research including cognitive-science foundations.

---

## 1. Framework Landscape

### 1.1 The Pyramid Principle and SCQA (Minto / McKinsey)

**What it is.** Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1960s, the Pyramid Principle inverts conventional analytical sequence: think bottom-up, communicate top-down. Three tiers:

- **Apex (Governing Thought):** A single complete-sentence claim — the recommendation or conclusion — not a topic label.
- **Middle (Supporting Arguments):** 3–5 MECE reasons answering "why?" Rule of Three is the practitioner default, grounded loosely in working-memory research.
- **Base (Evidence):** Data and analysis supporting each middle argument.

SCQA (Situation–Complication–Question–Answer) governs the *opening* of any deck within this system: Situation establishes shared context (nothing the audience disputes); Complication introduces what changed or is at risk; Question names the implicit inquiry this creates; Answer is the governing thought — delivered immediately, not at the end. [Deckary, Analyst Academy, StrategyU, Winning Presentations, ThinkInsights]

**Confidence: [high]** — practitioner consensus across 5+ independent primary sources; codified in Minto's published textbook (*The Pyramid Principle*, 1987/2002).

**Action-titles convention.** Every slide carries a complete declarative sentence as its title — the conclusion, not the topic. The "Read-Through Test": strip all charts; reading only the title sequence should reconstruct the full argument. This is how MBB partners review decks. [Slideworks, Deckary, Analyst Academy, Winning Presentations]

**Confidence: [high]**

**Documented failure modes:**

- Bloated Situation, weak Complication: over-explaining context without creating urgency. [Deckary, StrategyU]
- Topic-label titles: "Project update" carries no claim; "Project X is on schedule — one decision needed this week" does.
- More than 5 supporting arguments: signals unsynthesised thinking.
- Template-before-thinking: using SCQA as a fill-in form before the analysis is done produces a cosmetic structure around a missing governing thought. [Deckary]
- Fragile apex: if the audience immediately rejects the top-level recommendation, the whole support structure is vulnerable — no built-in resilience for adversarial settings. [ModelThinkers]
- Wrong context: top-down authoritative posture can damage trust in co-design or politically sensitive situations where stakeholders need ownership of the conclusion. [ModelThinkers]

**Confidence: [high]** for first three failure modes; **[moderate]** for fragile-apex and co-design caveats (fewer sources, more inferential).

### 1.2 Nancy Duarte — Resonate / Sparkline

**What it is.** A narrative-first framework derived from Joseph Campbell's monomyth and reverse-engineered from historically effective speeches (King, Jobs, Lincoln, Reagan). Its central contribution is the **Sparkline**: a presentation's emotional arc mapped as a time-series oscillating between:

- **"What Is"** — current, often uncomfortable reality
- **"What Could Be"** — a compelling vision of a transformed future

A presentation does not make one leap between planes; it "tacks" repeatedly, building cumulative persuasive tension. Each cycle widens the gap between present and possibility. The arc ends in **"The New Bliss"** — the destination the audience reaches by adopting the idea. [Duarte.com, Tomasz Tunguz, Sebastien Phlix, Six Minutes, Speaking About Presenting]

**Confidence: [moderate]** — framework is well-documented by the author; empirical validation is retrospective pattern analysis of famous speeches (survivorship-bias risk: unsuccessful speeches with the same structure are not studied).

**The audience-is-the-hero reframe.** "You are Yoda, not Luke Skywalker." The audience is the protagonist the presentation must transform; the presenter is the supporting mentor. This shifts content design from "what do I want to say?" to "what does my audience need at each moment?" [Duarte.com — Audience is Hero, Speaking About Presenting]

**Confidence: [moderate]** — practitioner consensus, widely cited, no controlled study.

**Executive-audience variant.** For C-suite, Duarte inverts her own narrative-build instinct: **lead with the conclusion**, not an arc build-up. The "Big Idea" must be one sentence stating (a) a unique point of view, (b) what is at stake for the audience, and (c) why they should care. Supporting detail becomes appendix; the meeting focuses on decisions. Send a "Slidedoc" (written pre-read) for complex material so meeting time is spent on decisions, not information transfer. [Duarte.com — Executive Presentation]

**Confidence: [high]** — Duarte is the primary source; consistent with McKinsey/consulting convention.

**S.T.A.R. moments.** Something They'll Always Remember: one or two high-point events per presentation using dramatisation, sound bites, striking visuals, stories, or statistics — designed to spike retention and anchor the key message. [Duarte.com]

**Confidence: [low]** — practitioner guidance, single primary source, no independent validation.

### 1.3 Garr Reynolds — Presentation Zen

**What it is.** A visual-storytelling framework grounded in Japanese Zen aesthetics (Kanso — simplicity; restraint; naturalness). Central claim: slides are not documents. The "slideument" — trying to make one artifact serve both functions — is the primary failure pattern of corporate presentation. [garrreynolds.com, ideasonstage.com]

**Confidence: [high]** — well-documented, multiple independent sources.

**Core flow principles:**

- **Conflict and stakes:** "No conflict, no story." Even non-adversarial talks need something at risk.
- **Reversals and surprise:** Questions that expose knowledge gaps, then answer them — a controlled discovery arc.
- **Hook immediately:** No opening formalities.
- **Chekhov's principle applied to slides:** Include only content that serves the narrative.

[presentationzen.com blog, ideasonstage.com, garrreynolds.com]

**Confidence: [moderate]**

**Analog ideation (pre-software storyboarding).** Reynolds insists on turning off the computer before opening any slide software. Use pen/paper or Post-it notes (one idea per note) to find structure before any design begins. The analog act bypasses template-constraint bias and enables non-destructive structural reordering. Only after the story structure is sound should slide production begin. [garrreynolds.com/preparation-tips, ideasonstage.com]

**Confidence: [moderate]** — practitioner consensus; the principle (storyboard before software) is independently echoed by Cliff Atkinson and by McKinsey's "Ghost Deck" practice.

**Reynolds vs. Minto — the core divide:**

| Dimension | Pyramid (Minto/McKinsey) | Presentation Zen (Reynolds) |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Decision clarity, efficient transfer | Emotional engagement, transformation |
| Opening | State conclusion first | Start with a hook, build the arc |
| Structure | Deductive top-down hierarchy | Narrative arc (problem → journey → resolution) |
| Tension | Eliminated by upfront resolution | Deliberate — holds attention |
| Slide unit | Argument node | One idea, one image, one moment |
| Visual style | Often text-dense, logic-forward | Full-bleed imagery, whitespace dominant |
| Audience role | Decision-maker wanting destination | Protagonist being moved to act |

[Winning Presentations, presentationzen.com, Slideworks]

**Confidence: [high]** — the divide is well-documented and the two frameworks are internally consistent.

---

### 1.4 Cliff Atkinson — Beyond Bullet Points

**What it is.** A screenwriting-derived method that treats slide software like a film script. Structure before slides: write a one-to-two-page Story Template (beginning/middle/end, inciting incident, call to action) before opening PowerPoint. Storyboard next. Slides are visual reminders for the speaker, not text repositories for the audience. [Microsoft Press Store]

**Confidence: [low]** — less independently cited in current literature; principle absorbed into other frameworks (Reynolds, McKinsey Ghost Deck) without attribution.

## 2. The Analytical vs. Narrative Divide

The two dominant frameworks encode opposite assumptions about what moves an executive audience:

**Pyramid/SCQA assumes** executives are decision-constrained and time-poor. They want the destination, not the journey. Answer-first is a courtesy and an efficiency. The structure's job is to eliminate ambiguity and make a decision maximally cheap to reach.

**Duarte/Reynolds assume** executives are humans first. Even in decision contexts, emotional resonance drives retention and follow-through better than pure logical efficiency. A recommendation that is analytically sound but emotionally inert will be approved and forgotten; one with narrative conviction will be remembered, advocated for, and executed with energy.

Both are right under different conditions. The practitioner literature converges on a hybrid: **analytical structure with narrative surface**. Use SCQA + Pyramid to ensure the argument is sound and the decision path is clear; use Duarte's emotional arc (sparkline oscillation, S.T.A.R. moments, audience-as-hero framing) to make the structure feel like a story rather than a report. Reynolds' visual and simplicity discipline applies to the slide layer regardless of which structural method drives the outline.

**Confidence: [moderate]** — this synthesis is [inference]; the hybrid is not explicitly described by any single source but is consistent with all five sources' own caveats and with Duarte's own executive-presentation guidance (which converges with Minto at the structural level).

## 3. Executive-Audience Specifics

### What executives want [high]

Executives want interpretation, not reconstruction. They process the highest volume of information of any audience; every non-essential slide or bullet is theft of attention. The consistent prescriptions across sources:

1. **State the recommendation or conclusion in the first sentence** — or on the first slide. (Duarte, McKinsey, SlideModel, Deckary)
2. **Make the ask explicit.** "I need X approved by Y date" — not implied. (DeCastro, Duarte, SlideModel)
3. **Limit key insights to 1–3** per session, each linked to a business outcome (revenue, risk, cost, competitive position). (SlideModel)
4. **The executive summary must stand alone.** All executives who stop reading after slide 2 should have received the complete argument. (McKinsey conventions, Deckary)
5. **Prepare for 50% of your allotted time.** Presentations to senior leaders are routinely interrupted. Plan for 15 minutes in a 30-minute slot. (DeCastro)

### Common failure modes [high]

The practitioner literature (DeCastro, Duarte, SlideModel, Deckary) converges on a closed failure set:

- **Information dump:** presenting everything you know; data belongs in an appendix unless challenged.
- **Making them read:** dense text-heavy slides transfer work to a busy audience.
- **Burying the lead:** rambling toward a conclusion. Executives interpret this as the presenter's lack of conviction.
- **No clear ask:** implicit action requests are not acted upon.
- **Filling the slot:** preparing exactly 30 minutes for a 30-minute meeting means the key point may never land if the meeting runs short.
- **Affectless delivery:** energy is contagious; conviction is necessary for executive audiences who are also evaluating whether to trust the presenter.

### Audience calibration by type [moderate]

| Audience | Primary lens | Consumption pattern | Key design implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Board** | Governance, risk, capital allocation | Non-linear; scans for "what changed" and "what decision" | Design for navigation, not linear reading |
| **Operational review** | KPIs, efficiency, accountability | Sequential; detail matters where it affects cost or growth | Full supporting evidence on slide; appendix for drill-down |
| **Approval/investment pitch** | Business model, ROI, market | Linear evaluation, fast (often 10–15 slides) | Kawasaki 10/20/30 as a brevity heuristic |

[StartupBOS, SlideModel, UMN HSJMC]

Note: the board vs. operational vs. pitch distinction is underdeveloped in the practitioner literature; most sources conflate "executive audience" into one category.

## 4. Cognitive Science Foundations

### Story activates more brain regions than data [moderate]

Stories simultaneously activate the prefrontal cortex (decision-making), hippocampus (memory formation), and limbic system (emotion). Dry statistics engage primarily language-processing regions. The multi-region activation of story-processing produces stronger memory encoding. [VeryBigBrain — neuroscience of storytelling; secondary synthesis source]

**Confidence: [moderate]** — the neuroimaging claim is broadly consistent with cognitive neuroscience literature but this source is secondary/popularising; the underlying studies are not directly cited.

### Processing fluency as the mechanism for narrative persuasion [moderate]

A 2021 peer-reviewed study in *Frontiers in Communication* identified **processing fluency** as the strongest mechanistic explanation for narrative persuasion: narratives feel cognitively easier to process than non-narrative presentations of the same information. That ease triggers positive associations and greater openness to persuasion — and this effect outperformed "identification with characters" as an explanatory mechanism. [Frontiers in Communication, 2021 — primary, peer-reviewed]

**Confidence: [moderate]** — single peer-reviewed study; finding is directionally consistent with practitioner literature but causal inference from one study is limited.

**Practical implication:** narrative structure (characters, complication, resolution) *reduces* cognitive load, freeing audience attention for content rather than consuming it on parsing structure. This is the cognitive-science basis for why slides-as-a-story outperform slides-as-a-report for retention and persuasion.

### The SUCCES model — what makes ideas "stick" [moderate]

Heath & Heath's *Made to Stick* (2007), based on a decade of analysis across urban legends, ad campaigns, and organizational communication, identified six principles for memorable ideas: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Story. The two most directly actionable for presentations:

- **Concrete:** sensory, specific, human-scale. "Saves 3 hours per analyst per week" outperforms "improves efficiency."
- **Story:** provides the context that makes data *mean* something rather than merely exist.

[Heath & Heath; Adapt Consulting secondary synthesis]

**Confidence: [moderate]** — practitioner consensus is strong; the underlying study methodology is not peer-reviewed in the academic sense.

### Counter-evidence: narrative can bypass critical evaluation [high]

The same persuasion research that supports narrative identifies a risk: compelling stories persuade even when the underlying data is weak, because narrative bypasses analytical evaluation. For executive audiences who are specifically trained to evaluate claims, a story-heavy deck without credible evidence can backfire. Credibility signals — data, expert endorsement, testable claims — remain load-bearing alongside narrative. [Frontiers in Communication, 2021; practitioner consensus]

**Confidence: [high]** — consistent across peer-reviewed and practitioner sources; named as a specific risk for analytically rigorous audiences.

## 5. The Ghost Deck / Storyboard-First Principle

A convergent technique appearing independently across McKinsey practice, Reynolds' analog ideation, and Atkinson's Story Template is: **build a text-only or sketch-level structure before producing any designed slides.**

- **McKinsey:** The "Ghost Deck" or "Dot-Dash Storyline" — slide titles plus minimal bullets, argument validated before any design. A 30-slide strategy deck typically takes 40–60 hours total, but the structural skeleton is pressure-tested at hours 2–4.
- **Reynolds:** Post-it storyboarding on a table, or a blank 12-slides-per-sheet thumbnail sketch in a notebook.
- **Atkinson:** A written Story Template (1–2 pages) before opening PowerPoint.

Common principle: slide software is a production container, not a thinking tool. Using it to draft structure embeds template-constraint bias before the argument is sound. [A1 Slides, garrreynolds.com/preparation-tips, Microsoft Press Store/Atkinson, ideasonstage.com]

**Confidence: [high]** — convergent independent sources.

## 6. Key Tension: When to Use Which Framework

| Context | Dominant method | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Executive decision deck (C-suite, board) | Pyramid + SCQA | Executives want destination; answer-first respects time constraints |
| Persuasive keynote / all-hands | Duarte Sparkline | Emotional arc and "what could be" vision move large audiences |
| Visual/product pitch or investor deck | Hybrid: Pyramid structure + Reynolds visual discipline | Clarity of argument + emotional simplicity of one-idea-per-slide |
| Training or educational deck | Reynolds + Atkinson | Learning requires narrative progression; not a decision context |
| Ambiguous / exploratory briefing | SCQA opening (establishes shared ground) + narrative middle | Establishes context before claiming conclusion |

**Confidence: [moderate]** — [inference] synthesised from per-framework context guidance in sources; no single source provides this calibration matrix.

## Known Unknowns

- **Known-unknown:** No peer-reviewed, controlled studies comparing Pyramid-structured decks against alternatives on measurable outcomes (decision speed, approval rates, executive satisfaction). The entire practitioner evidence base is consensus and case examples. Would be closed by: randomized experiment comparing SCQ/Pyramid vs. inductive narrative decks in a controlled executive-decision simulation.

- **Known-unknown:** The board vs. operational review vs. pitch calibration is underdeveloped in the primary literature. Most sources conflate executive audiences. Would be closed by: practitioner interviews or case studies segmented by audience type.

- **Unknowable (as currently posed):** Whether a hybrid Pyramid-structure/Sparkline-surface approach outperforms pure implementations of either. The frameworks were developed independently for different contexts; no comparative study exists, and the combination requires judgment calls that vary by presenter, audience, and content type.

- **Known-unknown:** Long-term retention effects of story-structured vs. data-structured executive presentations. Processing fluency research covers persuasion in the moment; post-meeting follow-through and decision implementation are not studied. Would be closed by: longitudinal study of executive decision execution rates by presentation format.
